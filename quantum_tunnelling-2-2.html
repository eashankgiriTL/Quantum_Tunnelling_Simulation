Physics Reference

Quantum tunnelling

A particle with \(E has a finite probability of crossing a classically forbidden barrier, a direct consequence of the wave nature of matter (de Broglie wavelength).

Why it happens

Heisenberg's uncertainty principle prevents simultaneous exact knowledge of position and momentum, so the wavefunction leaks exponentially into and through the barrier.

Inside the barrier

When \(E, \(\psi \propto e^{-\kappa x}\) where \(\kappa=\frac{\sqrt{2m(V_0-E)}}{\hbar}\). Larger mass or deeper energy deficit gives larger \(\kappa\) and stronger exponential suppression of \(T\).

Approx vs Exact

Approx: Ignores back-reflected wave inside barrier. Accurate when \(\kappa L \gg 1\) (equivalently \(\sinh(\kappa L)\gg 1\)). Can exceed \(1\) for thin barriers, which is unphysical.
Exact: Always gives \(0 \le T \le 1\).

Above barrier (E > V₀)

Classically all particles pass, but quantum mechanics predicts partial reflection. \(T\) oscillates with barrier width \(L\); resonance occurs when \(kL=n\pi\), giving \(T=1\).
